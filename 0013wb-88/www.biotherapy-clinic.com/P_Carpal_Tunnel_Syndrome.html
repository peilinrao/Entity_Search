Every year nearly 2 million people in the US suffer work-related musculoskeletal disorders, including repetitive strain injury (RSI), such as carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) and tendonitis caused by computer use.

Inside our wrist there is a narrow passage called the carpal tunnel that holds tendons, blood vessels, and the meridian nerve, all of which lie side by side, very close to one another. In general, injury or trauma to the area, including (but not limited to) repetitive movement of the wrists, can cause swelling of the tissues and carpal tunnel syndrome. This injury may be from sports or from sewing, typing, driving, assembly-line work, painting, writing, use of tools (especially hand tools or tools that vibrate), or similar activities.

It is well known from our daily life that narrow spots, such as tunnels, cause traffic congestion and accidents. A similar condition occurs in the wrist tunnel. When the tendons become inflamed or swollen, they squeeze the meridian nerve that runs to the fingers.

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS) and repetitive strain injury (RSI) have a range of similar symptoms: pain, numbness, a tingling or burning sensation, weakness or loss of grip strength, and loss of sleep due to pain and discomfort.

Some of the conditions associated with carpal tunnel syndrome in women include pregnancy, premenstrual syndrome (PMS), and menopause, probably because of hormone changes that cause fluid retention and swelling of the tissues. Carpal Tunnel Syndrome occurs most often in women 29 to 65 years old, but also in men and all age groups.

Other conditions associated with carpal tunnel syndrome include rheumatoid arthritis, renal failure, diabetes mellitus, hypothyroidism, and obesity, and unhealthy diet, excessive consumption of sweets, or highly processed foods, allergies, intestinal dysfunction, and fungal infection. Other factors stimulating the onset of Carpal Tunnel Syndrome are stress, especially emotional stress, nervousness, fear, depression, and sleeplessness.

Sometimes pain in the wrist or hand and other symptoms of Carpal Tunnel Syndrome may be caused by abnormal processes happening in the neck and upper spine that may affect the nerves running to the wrist. Muscle tension in the neck, shoulders, and hands may cause a deficiency of microcirculation in the soft tissues of the hand and accumulation of toxic metabolites that cause swelling of the tissues of the wrist.
