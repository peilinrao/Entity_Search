"GETTING THINGS RIGHT, GETTING THINGS DONE"

 

A Sermon by the Rev. Bruce Clear

Sunday, April 3, 2005

All Souls Unitarian Church

Indianapolis, Indiana

 

 

            I'm not sure when it started, but a life-lesson seems to visit me from time to time and knock me in the head and say, "do you get it now?"  That life lesson can be simply put:  "there are no final answers in life, and the learning is never finished.  So enjoy the learning."  This life-lesson is about giving up on getting everything right -- or maybe getting any one thing absolutely right -- and being excited about what can be accomplished. 

            I don't know when this lesson first hit me in the head.  But let me offer three very different stories, each of which illustrates a different aspect of this lesson.  None of these stories is profound.  Most are easily forgettable.  But each one I remember because it taught me a lesson about getting things right -- or more precisely about letting go of getting things right. 

My earliest memory of the lesson was probably when, as a teenager, I first heard the famous ancient Hindu parable about the blind men and the elephant.  Many of you may know the story, so I'll just summarize.  It tells of a group of blind men who are asked to describe what an elephant looks like.  Each one touches a different part of the elephant, so the one who touches a leg says the elephant is like a tree.  The one who touches the trunk says the elephant is like a large snake.  The one who touches the ear says the elephant is shaped like a gigantic palm leaf but made with leather.  And so on.  The lesson of the story, of course, is that even though they disagree among each other, all are right -- but only partially right -- with what they say.  The story also suggests that we may be like those blind men, unable to grasp the whole truth, though we are satisfied with our partial truths. 

            I heard that story in a Sunday School class, and I recall it having a big impact on me about religion.  From that moment on, I began to take everything I was taught with a grain of salt.  It also taught me something about tolerance of other religions -- that perhaps they are just seeing a different part of the elephant than I can see.  And it taught me some humility knowing that I am probably not seeing the whole truth. 

            The second story happened just a few years later when I was in college and I met, for the first and only time, a distant cousin of mine who held a fairly high government office in Washington.  Being a political science major in college, I was pretty confident that I knew all that I needed to know on the topic of politics and government, and I recall an exciting conversation.  This was decades ago, and there is only one piece of that conversation I still recall, but I have thought of it often.  At one point the topic turned to a political philosophy known as libertarianism.  Libertarians, many of you know, support a philosophy of keeping government as much as possible out of the lives of citizens.  This philosophy cuts across the political ideologies.  For example, libertarians oppose gun control, but support abortion rights -- both positions derive from the theory that it is none of the government's business whether we own guns or what we choose to do with our bodies. 

            Anyway, at some point in the conversation, I made a comment that though I can't fully subscribe to the libertarian view, I admire their consistency.  That is, their view of restricting government seems to be consistently applied to every issue that comes along.  My cousin responded with a lengthy silence, and then he said something like this:  "You know, I'm not sure that consistency is as important as a lot of other virtues." 

            It was a simple comment made thirty years ago in a long conversation about which I remember nothing else today.  But it took me so by surprise that I never forgot it.  Why isn't it more important to be, say, compassionate and charitable in our treatment of others than it is to be consistent in our ideas? 

            I suppose at the time I had either not heard or not understood Emerson's famous aphorism that "a foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds."  He's a little harsh in his words, but I think I know what he means.  Since then, I've not been afraid of a little inconsistency for fear that I might not be right. 

            Here's one more story of a different kind, but still addresses the lesson about the importance of being right.  Some years ago I was visiting a man in the hospital.  He had a rare intestinal disease in which he couldn't digest liquids.  So he was in the hospital indefinitely until they could figure out what to do.  He had already been there quite a while, with no end in sight. 

            In talking with him, he made a comment I will never forget.  He said something like this: 

 

"You know, I always avoided visiting people in the hospital.  It wasn't because hospitals bother me; it was because I never felt I could come up with the right things to say to them.  It scared me that I would say the wrong things.  But now, since I've been in the hospital, I've changed my thinking.  Now I know it doesn't matter at all what people say when they visit.  The only thing that matters is that they are there." 

 

            These very different stories share a common insight for me.  In each case, they served to make me question how important it is to get things right.  In each case we discover that there are, in fact, things more important than being right. 

 

            I also want to say some more about this idea of "getting things right."  In using that phrase, I hope you don't think I'm talking simply about what we mean when we say someone is a "perfectionist."  That is not what I mean.  A "perfectionist" is a person who always has to make sure every "i" is dotted and every "t" is crossed.  A perfectionist cannot abide sloppy work, and that is sometimes good.  But that is not what I mean by "getting things right." 

            What I mean by "getting things right" is the need we humans seem to have to know what the truth is about life.  We want to know for sure which choices are right when we decide on a job or a spouse or a house to buy or a church to attend.  We want to know that our choices are the right choices. 

We want to know whether it is right to be a Republican or a Democrat or an Independent.  We want to know for certain if there is or is not a God, whether there is or is not an afterlife.  We don't want mere speculation.  We want to be right.  We want to know the right way to raise a child.  We want to be certain about whether the death penalty is the right policy or the wrong one for our society.  We want to know if Jesus really did rise from the dead or if human beings have souls.  We want to know whether it was right to invade Iraq and whether privatization will save or harm the Social Security system.   On any question of any weighty nature, we just want to be right.  When it comes to the big questions of life, we don't want to get it wrong.  We want assurance that we are right.  Or at least we want to avoid being wrong. 

            I find this a fairly common feeling among us mortals.  And as a mortal myself, I share in that aspiration for being right.  In fact, I may experience that wish to be right more than most people, and that is why it is important for me to keep receiving lessons from life about why being right isn't always the most important part of life. 

            Life is about living, and sometimes insisting on being right seems to get in the way of life. 

 

            Some years ago, I was delighted to discover that there is, in fact, a distinct philosophical tradition that is rooted in this notion that we can't always expect to get things right.  That philosophy has been given the name of "pragmatism."  Interestingly, it is one of the very few distinctively American traditions, and its originated from the work of the early twentieth century thinkers John Dewey and William James. 

            The word "pragmatism" has become cloaked with some rather superficial meaning.  For most of us the word simply means finding the simplest way to solve a problem.  Someone who is "pragmatic" finds an efficient solution without becoming bogged down in subtle nuances of argument or meaning. 

            To illustrate the commonly accepted understanding of "pragmatism," I'm going to risk telling a joke I told a few years ago.  I re-tell it because first, it is appropriate, second, there may be quite a few people here today who weren't here a few years ago when I told it before, and third, it is one of the few jokes I've told from the pulpit that people actually thought was funny: 

During the French Revolution, three people were scheduled to be executed one day.  One was French, but the second was English and the third was an American. 

            The Frenchman went first, placing his neck on the slot below the guillotine blade.  The executioner asked if he had any last words, and the French prisoner yelled out, “Liberté, Egalité, Fraternité!”  The executioner released the blade, but as it descended it started slowing down with a squealing sound until it actually stopped an inch above his neck.  Those in attendance realized it was a sign that he should be freed, so the Executioner unbound him and let him go. 

            The second prisoner was English and when asked if he had any last words, he exclaimed, “God save the King!” whereupon he placed his neck below the blade and the executioner again released it.  As before, the blade mysteriously started slowing down until it grounded to a stop above the Englishman’s head.  It was another miracle!  The Englishman was released. 

            Then the American prisoner was escorted to the guillotine.  He too was asked whether he had any last words.  After thinking about it for a little while, he finally said to the executioner, “You know, if you put a little oil on that thing, it might work better.” 

            That illustrates, I think, our commonly understood meaning of someone who is a pragmatist.  By and large we say a pragmatist is an efficient problem solver. 

            That is not what James and Dewey meant by the term, nor those who follow in their tradition. 

            Pragmatism begins with the conviction that truth is ultimately illusive.  No matter how deeply we ponder the important questions of life, we cannot ultimately be certain of the answer.  One of John Dewey's books was titled "The Quest for Certainty," and his conclusion was that over centuries of human attempts to grasp truth with certainty, we have been unable to succeed.  Contemporary science has served to confirm this premise.  Philosophers of science point out that there is no scientific proposition that can be 100% certain.  All is theory.  Some theories work extraordinarily well when implemented, and some work less well.  But no theory rises to the level of certainty. 

            So if we begin with the acknowledgement that we can never know truth with certainty, what criteria do we use to make choices?  The answer is surprisingly simple, almost deceptively simple.  We look not so much for what is true, but more to the point, we look for what works.  And what do we mean by "what works?"  It means we look for what provides for the outcome or consequence that we wish for. 

            This, after all, is also the method science uses.  Experiments are designed to demonstrate whether a theory works.  If a theory doesn't work under such conditions, it is either discarded or revised.  The pragmatist says personal beliefs are likewise measured by what difference those beliefs make in a person's life.  The question of "truth" is by-passed, and we look, more importantly, to the consequences of that  belief. 

            By the way, this is the reason our ill-fated American friend at the guillotine was not a true pragmatist, in the sense I'm suggesting.  It seems he failed fully to examine the consequences of his choices and let those consequences guide his decision. 

William James in particular worked carefully on this idea of evaluating beliefs according to consequences rather than truth.  A belief has merit, he said, according to the practical differences that belief makes.  Take, for example, a belief in God.  The truth of that proposition will never be known.  What can be known is whether such a belief makes a difference in a person's life.  If believing in a God or not believing in a God makes a person more responsible and compassionate, that belief has a very practical meaning.  If a person's belief about God makes no difference in how they live, their belief has no practical meaning.  What a person believes to be true has little importance; the consequences of those beleifs can have substantial importance. 

            This insight can be applied to my opening stories.  Remember the man who was afraid to visit people in the hospital because he feared he couldn't find the right thing to say to the patient?  If we concede the fact that there is no such thing as "the right thing" and understand that what is "right" is what results in the best outcome, then we discover, as he did, that showing concern is more important than finding the right words. 

            Or revisit my cousin's critique of consistency, saying that being consistent may not necessarily be the greatest virtue.  He was critiquing the libertarian political philosophy that consistently applies to every political issue the principle of least government interference.   Its philosophy is much like the famous adage of Jefferson that "that government which governs best, governs least." 

            I continue to hold deep respect for the libertarians I know, yet I take a pragmatist view about consistency.  To apply the principle of the least government intrusion without evaluating the consequences of such policies in each case seems to me to make the virtue of "consistency of principle" more important than other values -- such as justice or equity or human welfare.  The pragmatist approach suggests that since we cannot be certain about any transcendent principle, our responsibility is to look at what works best according to the consequences we seek. 

            And then there's the parable of the blind men and the elephant.  The parable reminds us that our vision of the truth is limited.  But it also teaches us that we can benefit from learning the perspectives of others who see the world differently.  The consequence of believing that our own view of the elephant is the true view is that we miss the insights from other views.  The consequence of being skeptical about the ultimate truth of our view is that we welcome and benefit from the insights of others. 

            When we forego the need to get things right, in fact, we open ourselves up to greater personal growth.  And that growth comes from mutual learning from others. 

            Perhaps the most prominent philosophical voice today in defense of the pragmatic approach is Stanford philosopher Richard Rorty.  Rorty affirms the pragmatic traditional dismissal of the quest to find certain truth.  In its place, though, he offers a different recipe for life.  He calls it "conversation" -- the give and take of ideas, the incremental learning of communities.  Life, he says, is or ought to be an ongoing conversation.  This does not mean just verbal communication.  It is becoming engaged with others in such a way that your life becomes shaped by those encounters. 

            Life is about learning, about improving, but not about getting it finally right.  Life is an ongoing process of discovery, and this happens through constructive relationships with others.  Conversation is all those blind men listening to each other as they describe their perspectives.  We are in conversation whenever we allow ourselves to be challenged and re-awakened by our encounters with others. 

            We do not carry on that conversation in order to discover some truth and thereby end the conversation.  Rather, the conversation is the end in itself.  The consequence of conversation is continually adapting in new ways to the world that is always changing. 

            Out of several books by Rorty, there is one sentence that absolutely stung me, turned on a light, and stayed with me now for years.  This sentence provided another of those life-lesson stories like the stories I told earlier.  It is a fairly simple sentence: 

 

"In the end, the pragmatist tells us, what matters is our loyalty to other human beings clinging together against the dark, not our hope in getting things right." 

 

            The sentence may not startle you as it did, and still does, for me, but in that sentence I picture a Door Number One and a Door Number Two.  Door Number One has a sign that says "Helping each other successfully get through the hard times of life."  Door Number Two has a sign that says, "Being Right."  Which door would you choose?  We probably would not hesitate to pick the first one. 

            Getting things right or getting successfully and happily through life?  It would be good to be given such a choice, but we aren't.  The choice of getting things right is, for us mere mortals, an unachievable goal. 

 

            I confess I'm still working on this life lesson, and I haven't quite got it completely mastered.  I still feel this urge to be certain I'm right on things.  I appreciate whenever I stumble over something that knocks the lesson into my brain.

            The message of pragmatism is that "getting things right" is a futile and misguided goal.  One lesson from that message is that of continuous change and growth and transformation in life.  As we stay in conversation with others, our life changes.  We learn and never stop learning.  We change and never stop changing.  It is as if we continue becoming a new person as life draws us into a new chapter.  We learn and grow and change with life, never fully completing it, but seeking something better, leaving our past to a better future as we grow and change.
